What's the ideal Place to live in Maryland? (based on your needs)¶

We explore the question "What's the optimal area to live in Maryland based on your needs?". We answer this question based on four key factors that matters to most people:

  • Income
  • Crime rates
  • Weather

Each of these factors contributes to livability and varies significantly across different counties and cities in Maryland.

We're going tackle this problem actively using the process of the data lifecycle. This involves data curating to Exploratory Data analysis and hypothesis testing, using machine learning to help us make an inference, visualize our findings and then finally using our procedure and make an informed decision as to what's the optimal place to live based on your needs.

Income¶

Hypothesis Test: Is the User’s Income Significantly Different from the Average? We used a one-sample t-test to evaluate whether the user's income is significantly different from the average income across Maryland cities. Since the optimal city depends on user preferences, we created a default profile for this analysis. The default user's income is set at $60,000.

Null Hypothesis (H₀): The mean income of cities in Maryland equals the user's income. Alternative Hypothesis (H₁): The mean income of cities is different from the user's income.

Result: T-statistic = -20.85 P-value < 0.05

Conclusion: There is strong statistical evidence that the user's income is significantly different from the average income across Maryland cities. The T-statistic of -20.85 indicates that the average city income is far below the user's income—by over 20 standard errors. The P-value < 0.05 confirms that this difference is highly unlikely to be due to random chance.

How this helps the user: This result suggests that the user can afford to live in many cities across Maryland. By comparing the user's income to city-level incomes, we can filter out unaffordable locations and focus recommendations on cities where the cost of living (as proxied by income levels) is within reach. This test supports one part of our goal: helping users find cities that are economically suitable based on their income.

Primary analysis¶

We utilize this unsupervised machine learning technique to address the inherent complexity of simultaneously evaluating multiple critical factors such as income, crime rates, and weather conditions without relying on subjective judgments or labeled data.

K-Means clustering excels at discovering underlying patterns within complex, multi-dimensional datasets. By standardizing our chosen features (median income, violent crime rates, and average annual temperatures), we ensure all variables are given equal consideration. K-Means then systematically identifies clusters based on the similarities and dissimilarities among regions, generating distinct quality-of-life profiles. Each resulting cluster encapsulates a balanced combination of the factors, naturally grouping regions into meaningful categories such as "high income + moderate crime + warm climate" or "moderate income + low crime + cool climate."

Critically, clustering simplifies the task of personalized recommendation. When a user specifies their preferences, for instance, desiring high income opportunities, low crime, and mild weather, these preferences are translated into the same standardized scale used for clustering. K-Means efficiently identifies the cluster whose centroid most closely matches the user's criteria, instantly narrowing down potential regions.

Once clusters are created, we can retrieve the top regions by selecting those points closest to the centroid matching the user's preferences, ensuring that the recommendations are directly aligned with what matters most to each user. Subsequently, regions within this targeted cluster can be precisely ranked according to their proximity to the user's specific preferences, providing clear, actionable recommendations.

This approach is computationally efficient, as clustering only needs to be performed once, after which individual user queries require minimal computational resources. It also ensures transparency and interpretability, as each cluster can be described clearly and intuitively to end-users, aiding their decision-making process. Additionally, visualizing the clusters through techniques such as PCA plots further strengthens user engagement and understanding, making complex multi-dimensional data accessible and easily comprehensible.

In essence, employing K-Means clustering aligns seamlessly with our project's goal of providing data-driven, user-centric recommendations. It effectively addresses the multidimensional nature of choosing optimal living locations and delivers personalized, transparent, and reliable insights to our users.
